My GCSP Theme
"Sustainability doesn't just stop at the environment: it extends to designing technology that preserve resources, access, and opportunity for the future."

Sustainability is the overarching theme, but it shows up differently across each competency. Sometimes it's literal: building a solar-powered irrigation system that prevents water waste on community farms, or planting trees to restore urban green infrastructure. Other times, it is in preserving computational resources, human time, and equitable access to technology. Password policies that are infeasible for people of all backgrounds/wastes GPU resources is unsustainable. A VR platform that replaces repeated physical testing environments is a more resource-conscious way to do research. No matter the project, I ask the same core question: what are we consuming, and how do we preserve more of it?

Research or Major Project — depth in a Grand Challenge area

How This Connects to Sustainability

VR and ML are sustainability tools in disguise. Rather than repeating physical experiments in real manufacturing spaces: consuming materials, energy, and time, a VR digital twin lets researchers simulate and study the same environment indefinitely within a single platform. My ML model for dementia detection takes this further: instead of requiring repeated, resource-intensive clinical evaluations, it automates cognitive screening from eye-tracking data collected in VR. Both projects are about preservation: preserving physical resources, clinical capacity, and researcher time by building smarter, and human centric systems.

Project 1 · NSF REU, University of Louisville

VR Digital Twin for Manufacturing

Developed a virtual reality environment in Unity using Photon Fusion to simulate a digital twin of a collaborative manufacturing system. Collected eye-tracking and user-interaction data to study task performance, applied statistical methods (t-tests, Spearman correlation) to evaluate usability and cognitive load, and proposed data-driven design improvements co-authored in a journal paper and presented at HFES 2026.

Global perspective — understanding challenges across contexts

How This Connects to Sustainability

Sustainable technology must also be equitable. My multicultural experiences taught me that the systems we build (especially in security) carry hidden costs when they exclude or disadvantage users. Outdated password policies based on outdated rules cause massive GPU waste and lead to brute-force attacks, high reset rates, and repeated failed authentication. They also dissuade users from caring about security. Participating in spaces like Grace Hopper and ACM-W showed me that sustainability in computing means building for diverse users from the start (ex. people of all ages and backgrounds) because exclusionary systems always waste more, in energy and in human potential.

ML Framework for Password Security

Conducted research under Dr. Shiva Houshmand on how ML can modernize password strength evaluation. Fine-tuned a BERT model on 468K labeled passwords to classify strength beyond rigid LUDS rules, achieving 87% accuracy. Competency Three

Interdisciplinary

Connecting fields of engineering, humanities, science & society

How This Connects to Sustainability

The Hydration Automation project is sustainability in its most direct form, but it took an interdisciplinary team to make it work. Engineers alone couldn't have understood the farming community's needs; business school collaborators shaped the market framing; CAS perspectives informed how we communicated with Veggielution. The system itself was built for preservation: solar-powered infrastructure, LoRa radio for low-power wireless transmission, and real-time soil moisture data to prevent over-irrigation and nutrient leaching. Every design decision asked: what can we eliminate, reuse, or reduce?

Research Project · EPIC Lab & Frugal Innovation Hub · IEEE EPICS Funded

Hydration Automation: LoRa-based Smart Irrigation for Community Farms

At the Ethical, Pragmatic, and Intelligent Computing Laboratory under Dr. Navid Shaghaghi (and the Frugal Innovation Hub), I helped design and deploy a cost-effective smart irrigation platform for urban community farms. I developed a RESTful API (Node.js/Express) to log IoT sensor data and a React/Next.js dashboard with real-time soil moisture visualization. The system runs on solar-powered LoRa nodes at under $75/node — a fraction of commercial alternatives ($500–$2,500+).

The team spans business, engineering, and CAS — truly cross-disciplinary. Presented at IEEE MIT URTC (2025), IEEE GHTC (2025), and Stanford TETHICON (2026). Deployment is live at Veggielution Community Farm in San José, CA with 8+ sensing units in the field. I also completed the Digital Age Pathway and hold double minors in Economics and Mathematics — both non-engineering disciplines that directly inform market analysis and quantitative modeling.

How This Connects to Sustainability

Good sustainable technology only matters if it can reach the people who need it. Entrepreneurship is the bridge. The smart irrigation system was designed from the ground up to be economically viable for community farms — under $75/node with no recurring software fees, compared to $1,500–$2,500/year from commercial competitors. My work at the Ciocca Center taught me how to evaluate whether an idea is not just technically sound, but economically durable. Sustainability and entrepreneurship share the same core discipline: building things that last.

Project · Frugal Innovation Hub

Smart Irrigation Deployment at Veggielution

Created and deployed a low-cost smart irrigation system to Veggielution, a community farm in San José. Conducted literature reviews and market analysis benchmarking against Rain Bird, Hunter, Tektelic, and Tevatronic — demonstrating that the system delivers wireless, solar, modular precision irrigation at ~3% of the cost of nearest competitors. A direct translation of frugal engineering into a viable, sustainable product.
