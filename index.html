Design Principles and Usability Analysis of Virtual Reality Manufacturing Digital Twins
Aqlan, F., Kabadi, S. — MDPI Systems Journal, 2025 · Poster: HFES 70th International Annual Meeting 2026
In Preparation
Poster Presentation — IMECE 2025
NSF REU Research, University of Louisville
Accepted
Introduction & Background

Virtual Reality (VR) has emerged as a powerful medium for immersive training and real-time system monitoring in manufacturing environments. Digital Twins (DTs) extend this capability by providing continuous, bidirectional synchronization between a physical system and its virtual counterpart — enabling operators to visualize, monitor, and control manufacturing processes remotely. Despite their promise, the usability of VR-based Digital Twin (VRDT) systems is frequently overlooked, and poor interface design can significantly diminish their effectiveness in real-world deployments.

This project was conducted at the University of Louisville as part of an NSF-funded Research Experience for Undergraduates (REU) program. The physical twin consisted of collaborative robots, 3D printers, conveyors, sensors, and real-time monitoring devices. The digital twin replicated this collaborative manufacturing system in Unity, enabling immersive visualization, interaction, and real-time synchronization via the ZeroMQ messaging framework. Eye-tracking, facial expression, physiological, and interaction log data were collected to produce a multi-modal dataset for usability analysis.

Grand Challenge Connection: VR Digital Twins support sustainability by eliminating the need to run repeated physical experiments — reducing material consumption, energy use, and infrastructure costs associated with physical manufacturing tests.

Methodology

System Architecture

The VRDT was built in Unity and connected to the physical manufacturing system using the ZeroMQ framework for real-time bi-directional data transfer. Users wore a VR headset equipped with eye-tracking (gaze fixation, pupil dilation) and facial expression capture. The system logged environment sensor data (temperature, pressure, humidity, platform location, extruder movement) alongside operator commands, video recordings, and physiological signals (electrodermal activity, blood vessel pulse).

Experiment Design

Participants completed a series of tasks within the VRDT including robot control, 3D printer operation, and part handling in both virtual and physical contexts. Post-task surveys collected System Usability Scale (SUS) scores and SIM-TLX cognitive workload ratings. Demographic data and VR familiarity were recorded to support subgroup analysis. A total of 22 participants completed the study.

Data analysis applied Spearman correlation between SUS and SIM-TLX scores, K-Means clustering on interaction log data (PCA-reduced), and item-level SUS analysis to identify specific interface pain points. T-tests evaluated usability differences across age groups and VR familiarity levels.

The mean SUS score of 75.3 indicated good overall usability and positive user acceptance of the VRDT system. No significant usability differences were observed across age groups, suggesting the interface was broadly accessible. However, VR familiarity was a significant factor: users with higher prior VR experience reported substantially lower cognitive workload and higher usability scores. The Spearman correlation between SUS and SIM-TLX (r = −0.58, p = 0.005) confirmed that higher perceived workload was associated with lower usability — particularly for Tasks 3, 10, and 12 where consistent difficulty patterns indicated potential interface-level issues.

K-Means clustering on interaction log data (PCA-reduced) identified three distinct user groups: high-usability, moderate, and low-usability. Users in the low-usability cluster showed excessive repeated interactions, suggesting interface confusion and inadequate feedback mechanisms. Strong immersion and presence were reported across all groups, while task control was rated lower — indicating that spatial navigation and control mapping need refinement.

Discussion

The results confirm that the VRDT system achieves good overall usability, but that cognitive workload and VR familiarity are significant mediators of the user experience. Participants who were unfamiliar with VR struggled more with navigation and control, not necessarily because the tasks were difficult, but because the VR interaction paradigm itself introduced a learning overhead. Item-level SUS analysis showed that users found the system reasonably easy to learn with well-integrated features, though a subset perceived interface complexity and required additional guidance.

The weak correlation between interaction frequency and perceived usability/workload suggests that raw engagement is not a reliable usability indicator — what matters more is whether interactions are purposeful. Excessive repeated interactions by some users pointed to feedback gaps in the VRDT interface, where users were uncertain whether their commands had been registered.

Conclusions & Future Recommendations

The VRDT system demonstrated that immersive virtual reality can serve as an effective platform for manufacturing interaction and monitoring tasks, achieving good usability scores and strong immersion. Cognitive workload and VR familiarity are the primary factors to address in future iterations. Key design recommendations include onboarding guidance for VR-novice users, clearer feedback mechanisms for command confirmation, and adaptive interface complexity based on user familiarity level.

Future work should focus on: (1) enhancing system usability through targeted interface improvements informed by the SUS item-level analysis, (2) integrating data management and analytics pipelines for longitudinal monitoring, (3) advancing adaptive VR system design that responds to real-time workload indicators, and (4) expanding sample size and demographic diversity to strengthen generalizability.

Limitations: Limited sample size (N=22) and demographic diversity; potential simulation sickness confounding workload scores; time-intensive VRDT development and debugging process limiting iteration speed.

ML Framework for Password Policies
Kabadi, S., Houshmand, S. — ACMW Cascadia Computing Conference, 2025
Accepted
Introduction & Background

Password bias is a phenomenon where users create passwords using keywords referencing personal information, current events, cultural references, or easily memorized sequences. Psychologically, impulsivity, familiarity bias, locus of control, and limited cybersecurity knowledge all contribute to weak password practices. The consequences are severe: in 2018, the City of Atlanta suffered a ransomware attack after attackers brute-forced their way into city networks, ultimately causing millions in recovery costs and disrupting critical municipal services including law enforcement, utilities, and court systems.

Current password strength frameworks rely on the LUDS mechanism — lowercase, uppercase, digits, and symbols — which research has consistently criticized as ineffective. Simply requiring one symbol or digit does not change the fundamental guessability of a password. The zxcvbn library (Dropbox, 2016) improves on LUDS by encoding keyboard proximity, dictionary substitution, and entropy heuristics, but still relies on fixed rules rather than learning from data. This project introduces a machine learning framework using a fine-tuned BERT model as a richer, contextual alternative to both approaches.

Grand Challenge Connection: Poorly designed password policies produce unsustainable security systems — requiring massive GPU resources for brute-force cracking, driving up energy consumption, and generating excessive authentication overhead through high reset rates and failed logins. ML-informed policies can reduce this computational waste while improving security for all users.

Methodology

Data Preparation

The training dataset was sourced from Kaggle and contained 468,747 labeled passwords classified as weak (0), medium (1), or strong (2): comprising 62,791 weak, 347,760 medium, and 58,196 strong examples. After cleaning NaN values, a 70% stratified sample was taken to manage computational constraints. The model was trained on 70% of this sample with 30% reserved for testing. Two additional unlabeled test sets were prepared: 10,000 commonly used passwords and 10,000 randomly generated password strings.

Model Architecture & Training

The bert-base-cased model was selected from HuggingFace's Transformers library. The case-sensitive variant was chosen deliberately because letter casing is a meaningful signal in password strength. A linear classification layer was added on top of the BERT pooled output to produce 3-class predictions. Passwords were tokenized to a maximum length of 512 tokens with padding/truncation. The model was trained for 3 epochs using AdamW (lr = 2e-5) with a batch size of 8, and tracked cross-entropy loss across iterations.

After training, BERT was tested on both unlabeled datasets. Outputs were analyzed using correlation heatmaps (Pearson), bar charts of average feature values per strength class, and K-Means clustering on the random password set to identify structural patterns. BERT's classifications were then compared to zxcvbn using a confusion matrix, after mapping zxcvbn's 5-point scale to BERT's 3-point scale.

The fine-tuned BERT model achieved 87% accuracy and an F1 score of 0.81 on the test set — confirming that a language model can reliably learn structural and contextual patterns that distinguish password strength classes. On randomly generated passwords, Pearson correlation analysis showed password length (r = 0.85) and symbol count (r = 0.57) as the strongest predictors of high-strength classifications. Digit count showed moderate correlation (r = 0.42), while uppercase letter count also showed positive correlation (r = 0.52).

K-Means clustering of weak passwords revealed homogeneous clusters with low digit counts and few uppercase letters — weak passwords are structurally similar to one another. Strong passwords formed diverse, spread clusters with more varied feature combinations. Comparing BERT to zxcvbn via confusion matrix showed strong overall agreement: both models classified 2,871 passwords as weak and 4,070 as strong. Disagreements were concentrated in the medium-strength range, where BERT applies stricter contextual requirements than zxcvbn's rule-based heuristics.

Discussion

The results confirm that LUDS features — while often criticized as insufficient — still correlate meaningfully with password strength when analyzed contextually. The key insight is that BERT doesn't just count characters: it processes the bidirectional relationships between all characters in the password string, learning patterns that overlap significantly with what zxcvbn encodes through hand-crafted rules. The agreement between the two systems in weak and strong classifications, with divergence in the medium range, suggests that the borderline cases are genuinely ambiguous — and that ML can offer a more nuanced and adaptive judgment in that space.

The sustainability angle is reinforced by the clustering findings: weak passwords being structurally homogeneous means they're highly susceptible to dictionary and brute-force attacks, which run at massive computational cost. A better strength evaluator at the point of creation could prevent a large proportion of these attacks before they begin.

Conclusions & Future Recommendations

This project demonstrates that fine-tuned language models can effectively classify password strength, capturing contextual and structural patterns beyond what rule-based systems encode. The strong agreement between BERT and zxcvbn validates the approach while the divergence in medium-strength cases points to where ML adds the most value. The framework provides a methodological contribution to the password security community and opens a path toward data-driven, adaptive password policies.

Future work should explore: (1) fine-tuning RoBERTa (BERT's successor) and comparing results to bert-base-cased, (2) experimenting with more training epochs and different learning rates to optimize performance, (3) extending the framework to real-world leaked password datasets for adversarial robustness testing, and (4) integrating the model into a live password policy tool for institutional deployment.

Advisor: Dr. Shiva Houshmand, Santa Clara University. Published as a poster at the 2025 ACM-W Cascadia Computing Conference.

Hydration Automation: LoRa-based Smart Irrigation for Community Farms
Kabadi, S. et al., Shaghaghi, N. — IEEE MIT URTC 2025 · IEEE GHTC 2025 · Stanford TETHICON 2026
Presented
Introduction & Background

Urban farms face persistent, overlapping challenges: excessive water consumption from schedule-based irrigation without real-time soil sensing, high labor costs from manual monitoring, and vendor lock-in from commercial solutions priced far beyond the reach of non-profit and community organizations. Commercially available smart irrigation nodes (METER TEROS 12, GroPoint, Tektelic Intellidrip) range from $250 to over $2,500 per node, require AC power infrastructure, and carry proprietary cloud subscriptions. For community farms serving low-income populations, these systems are inaccessible.

The Hydration Automation (HA) project was developed at the Ethical, Pragmatic, and Intelligent Computing (EPIC) Laboratory and the Frugal Innovation Hub at Santa Clara University, under the guidance of Dr. Navid Shaghaghi. The project is funded through IEEE EPICS and deployed in partnership with Veggielution Community Farm in San José, CA — a community farm serving the East Side of San José. The core research goal was to deploy modular, solar-powered irrigation sensing under $80/node with zero recurring software fees.

Grand Challenge Connection: Schedule-based irrigation without soil sensing causes systematic over-irrigation and nutrient leaching. HA directly addresses water conservation and resource efficiency by providing community farms with data-driven, autonomous irrigation control at a fraction of commercial cost.

Methodology

Hardware Design

Each Sensing Unit (SU) consists of a PETG-encased Adafruit Feather M0 (ATSAMD21 ARM Cortex-M0+, 48 MHz) with an RFM9x LoRa radio module. Up to 4 capacitive soil moisture sensors per unit measure volumetric water content via dielectric permittivity at multiple depths. Power is provided by a mini solar panel trickle-charging a 3.7V LiPo battery, enabling indefinite off-grid deployment with no AC hookup. The custom PETG enclosure provides UV resistance and IP-rated weather protection, with open-source design files for reproducibility.

Network & Backend Architecture

SUs transmit soil data wirelessly to a Wi-Fi Base Station (BS) via LoRa Sub-GHz LPWAN links supporting a multi-SU star topology. The BS (Adafruit WINC1500, 802.11 b/g/n) aggregates LoRa packets and relays JSON records to a cloud backend via TLS-encrypted HTTPS POST. The backend is built on Express.js with a MongoDB time-series database. Authentication uses Google OAuth 2.0. The full-stack dashboard (Next.js/React, TypeScript, Tailwind CSS, Chart.js, Mapbox GL) provides real-time soil moisture visualization with geolocation and historical trend analysis.

Sensor firmware handles duty-cycle sleep scheduling to minimize power draw, with wake cycles triggered at configurable intervals. The team conducted iterative co-design sessions with Veggielution farmers to align dashboard usability with their monitoring workflows, refining data presentation and alert thresholds based on farmer feedback.

The system was successfully deployed with 8+ sensing units validated at Veggielution Community Farm under continuous field conditions. Competitive analysis demonstrated that HA delivers wireless, solar-powered, modular precision irrigation at approximately 3–5% of the cost of the nearest commercial competitor (Tektelic Intellidrip at $1,500–$2,500/yr per node). Unlike incumbent systems (Rain Bird, Hunter Irrigation), HA requires no wired AC infrastructure or proprietary controllers, making it viable for farms without existing electrical access in growing beds.

Real-time dashboard deployment enabled Veggielution farmers to monitor soil moisture across multiple growing beds simultaneously. The system correctly logged sensor readings and relayed them to the cloud dashboard during field validation trials. Co-design feedback from farmers led to dashboard improvements including simplified moisture threshold indicators and mobile-responsive layouts for use during farm walkthroughs.

Discussion

The project demonstrates that frugal engineering principles — designing for lowest possible cost without sacrificing core functionality — can produce systems that are both technically capable and practically deployable in resource-constrained settings. The interdisciplinary nature of the team was central to this outcome: engineering knowledge provided the hardware and software architecture, economics and business perspectives shaped the market positioning and cost analysis, and community engagement skills enabled effective co-design with Veggielution farmers.

The choice of LoRa radio over Wi-Fi for sensor-to-base-station communication was a key sustainability decision: LoRa's sub-GHz spectrum supports long range at extremely low power, allowing SUs to operate indefinitely on solar-charged batteries. This eliminates the need for wired power infrastructure in growing fields, reducing installation cost and environmental disruption significantly.

Conclusions & Future Recommendations

Hydration Automation demonstrates that a fully autonomous, solar-powered, LoRa-connected precision irrigation platform can be deployed under $75/node without sacrificing wireless range, cloud connectivity, data security, or modularity. Live deployment at Veggielution validates the design under real agricultural conditions, and the zero-recurring-fee model makes the system economically sustainable for non-profit farm operators.

Future directions include: (1) ML-based adaptive irrigation scheduling trained on historical volumetric water content data combined with NOAA weather API inputs, replacing fixed thresholds with predictive models; (2) closed-loop valve actuation integrating solenoid valves with SU firmware for fully automated irrigation control; (3) multi-farm deployment and A/B trials to quantify water savings (L/day) vs. schedule-based baselines; and (4) open-source release of CAD files, PCB schematics, and firmware to enable community replication.
